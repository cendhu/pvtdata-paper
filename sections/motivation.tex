\section{Motivation for Data Privacy}
\label{motivation}
Enterprise blockchain networks span diverse domains---supply chains, healthcare, trade finance---where organizations must share data for coordination while protecting proprietary or regulated information. We illustrate the privacy challenge through two representative use cases.

\textbf{Food Traceability.}
In 2006, a major \textit{E. coli} outbreak linked to bagged spinach infected 199 people across 26 U.S. states~\cite{ecoil}. Regulators took two weeks to trace the contamination source, during which significant quantities of safe food were unnecessarily wasted~\cite{blockchain-for-business}. A blockchain-based traceability system addresses this by providing a tamper-evident, shared record---Walmart demonstrated that blockchain could reduce backtrace time from seven days to 2.2 seconds~\cite{7-to-2, walmart-hf}. However, recording all transaction details (invoices, pricing, customer lists) on a replicated ledger \textit{leaks proprietary business information} to every participant, including competitors. Food standards authorities~\cite{fs, eu-foodsafety, fao-traceability} mandate detailed record-keeping, yet much of this data is commercially sensitive. Organizations face a dilemma: accept privacy violations, or keep sensitive data off-chain and sacrifice the consistency and auditability that motivated blockchain adoption.

\textbf{Personal Data Management.}
In healthcare, patients see an average of 18.7 different practitioners in their lifetime~\cite{practice-fusion}, yet Electronic Medical Records remain siloed in hospital-centric systems. A blockchain solution could give patients ownership of their records and enable instant verification by third parties (insurers, pharmacies). But directly storing medical diagnoses or treatment histories on a replicated ledger is unacceptable---legal mandates like the HIPAA Privacy Rule~\cite{hipaa-privacy} require providers to protect patient confidentiality, and regulations such as the GDPR~\cite{gdpr} impose strict controls on personal data processing.

These use cases highlight a common pattern: blockchain provides the trust and coordination benefits enterprises need, but its inherent transparency is incompatible with the fine-grained data privacy that real-world deployments require. This motivates our work on private data collections, which we introduce in the following section.
