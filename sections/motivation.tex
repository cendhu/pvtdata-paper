\section{Motivation for Data Privacy}
\label{motivation}
In this section, we motivate the need for providing data privacy in a blockchain network by introducing
the following three use-cases: (1) food traceability, (2) patient-centric health-care, and (3) identity
documents and certificates management. Each of these use-cases solves a real-world problem using blockchain
technology. However, due to the lack of support for data privacy in existing blockchain technologies,
some sensitive information could be leaked. For achieving the widespread adoption of blockchain, it is
necessary to introduce a mechanism to preserve the privacy of sensitive data.
\subsection{Food Traceability}
In September 2006, there was an outbreak of food-borne illness caused by Escherichia coli
bacteria~\cite{ecoil} in 26 U.S. states. This outbreak was linked to bagged spinach. By October 6, 2006, 199 people had been
infected, including three people who died and 31 who suffered kidney failure. It took regulators two
weeks to conduct the backtrace and determine the exact source of the outbreak. During those two weeks,
many people got sick, and tons of good spinach was unnecessarily discarded---wrongfully
wasted because we could not tell the good spinach from the bad~\cite{blockchain-for-business}.
An efficient food traceability mechanism is the key to reduce the backtrace time in such a scenario.
Traceability is the ability to track and record the movement of food products throughout a supply chain, which
includes growing, harvesting, packing, manufacturing, repacking, storage, distribution, and sale to the consumer.
With this information, it is possible to trace backward in the supply chain (usually to find the source of a
problem) as well as trace forward (usually to do a recall of contaminated products).

Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ)~\cite{fs}, European Commission's food safety policy~\cite{eu-foodsafety},
and Food and Agriculture Organization of United Nations (FAO)~\cite{fao-traceability} provide various guidelines on what
records need be kept in order to enable food traceability. The following are some of the records:
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=0pt,noitemsep,leftmargin=15pt]
    \item the name and address (and other contact details) of farmers/suppliers and a description of products.
	\item the name and contact information of the logistics providers and a description of products transported.
	\item the name and addresses (and other contact details) of customers and a description of the product supplied to them along with the product ID.
	\item date of transaction or delivery.
	\item batch or lot identification (or other markings such as product ID).
    \item volume or quantity of product supplied or received.
	\item raw materials used.
	\item conservation and storage methods for the product.
\end{enumerate}
As a result, when an outbreak of food-borne illness occurs, it would be possible to traceback from the customer
$\rightarrow$ the product ID $\rightarrow$ the supplier/farmer. Similarly, a recall can be performed by tracing forward
from the farmer/supplier $\rightarrow$ the product lot $\rightarrow$ customers. If these records are kept in the form of
papers or stored digitally in a centralized database maintained at individual operators, during an outbreak, a lot of
communication needs to happen between various food business operators to perform traceback or trace forward. Such a method
is very inefficient, time-consuming, and expensive. Hence, a blockchain-based food traceability
solution is needed for food safety. Further, such a solution helps to improve the overall supply chain efficiency, to
allow us to easily communicate information that customers are asking for (i.e., fork to farm), to handle disputes quickly,
and to help authenticate claims made in a product. With blockchain, network members can track the food products as they
travel from farm to fork. Recently, Walmart, a retailer, did an experiment that traced the origin of sliced mangos from
Walmart stores back to the farm. This process showed a radical improvement from approximately seven days it took to
conduct the backtrace by using traditional methods down to 2.2 seconds~\cite{7-to-2, walmart-hf} using Hyperledger Fabric.
\par
Figure~\ref{fig:fsc} shows actors involved in a simplified chocolate bars supply chain. For manufacturing a
chocolate bar, cacao and milk are required (assume a bar of sugar-free chocolate). Usually, manufacturers
buy raw materials from suppliers rather than directly contacting various farmers. Once chocolate bars are manufactured,
it would be sent to various retailers across the globe via distributors so that retailers can sell the chocolate bars to
customers. Note that distribution involves import/export authorities, customs, and other entities. For simplicity,
we do not cover all involved actors.
\par
\textbf{Blockchain-Based Solution.} Figure~\ref{fig:tx-flow-fsc} shows a sample business process in a chocolate supply
chain network. The process involves order placement, invoice creation, shipping arrangement, delivery, and proof of payment.
We can implement the whole process using a permissioned blockchain platform with the help of smart-contracts. As each
transaction is recorded in a replicated ledger, it enables the user to (a) perform the forward and backward tracing
quickly and efficiently; (b) resolve disputes quickly; (c) maintain a tamper-evident record management for food traceability.
Note that blockchain also helps in standardizing the business process and data format through smart-contract.
\par
\textbf{Data Privacy Violation.}\textit{
		Storing all information related to the above mentioned business process in a replicated ledger could leak
proprietary or business competitive information. For example, invoice details cannot be shared as it could leak
the business contract between the farmer and the supplier or the distributor and the retailer. As a result, a manufacture,
a distributor and a retailer can find out the profit made by the supplier, manufacture and the distributor, respectively.
This would result in unnecessary bargaining and conflicts between business partners.}

\subsection{Patient-Centric Health-Care}
The health-care domain faces the following challenges, which can be solved by using blockchain:
(1) noninteroperable Electronic Medical Records (EMR) management system; (2) a lack of transparency and trust;
(3) insurance fraud. 
\par
\textbf{Noninteroperable EMR system.} In health-care, a practitioner does not have access to all
the historical medical records of a patient. This is because the Electronic Medical Record (EMR) systems
assume that a patient sees only practitioners in one hospital. However, the reality is that, on average,
a patient in the US sees 18.7 different practitioners/hospitals during his/her lifetime~\cite{}. As existing
EMR systems are hospital-centric, there is no interest to interoperate or share data with other hospitals
due to competitions. As a result, the patient's EMR are trapped in different islands (on average 19 different
locations~\cite{}), which creates a hurdle to execute personalized health-care.
\par
\textbf{Lack of Transparency \& Trust.} A hospital-centric delivery model using the current framework of existing
technologies results in a lack of transparency and trust. A practitioner can provide a wrong treatment and
later tamper the EMR to hide his mistakes, given that the EMR system is managed at his/her hospital.
Moreover, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) states that patients should own
their medical records and control access permission.
\par
\textbf{Insurance Frauds.} There is a growing concern in the insurance industry on increasing
number of health-care insurance frauds. The FBI estimates that health care fraud costs American taxpayers \$80
billion a year~\cite{}. The following are the commonly committed frauds. (1) duplicate and inflated bills;
(2) manipulating pre-policy health check-up records; (3) inflated billing, billing for services not provided;
(4) unwarranted procedures, excessive investigations, expensive medicines;
(5) overutilization, extended length of stay; (6) fudging records, patient history.
\par
\textbf{Blockchain-Based Solution.} To resolve the above mentioned problems, we need a blockchain based solution,
as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:health-care}.
Here, the patient owns their EMR and controls access permission using the blockchain wallet. Hospitals can
create EMR for patients as a result of some medication or treatment. As the information is shared using a
replicated and immutable ledger, it adds more transparency \& trust, enables sharing of EMR records, maintains
complete provenance and audit trails and reduces the number of fraudulent events in the insurance industries.
Further, the smart-contract helps in standardizing the EMR data format so that sharing and processing of the
data become simpler.
\par
\textbf{Data Privacy Violation.} \textit{Sharing of sensitive data
		such as EMR of a patient in a replicated ledger is usually unacceptable as it leaks private information about
		a patient. Further, it violates laws such as HIPAA privacy rule~\cite{}, which dicdate the hospital to protect
		the patient's privacy.}

\subsection{Identity and Diploma Certificate Management}
\textbf{Identity Frauds.}
In 2018, nearly 14.4 million people in the US were impacted by some form of identity theft according to a survey conducted by
Javelin Strategy and Research~\cite{}. Such frauds costed \$1.7 billion in total for victims. The stoled identities were used
to takeover existing accounts and open new accounts for loan and mortages, which results in a financial loss for the victims.
According to interpol~\cite{}, criminals make fake identities to carry out their illegal activities. Criminals use counterfeit and
forged identity or travel document as well as genuine identity or travel document which was acquired using other
counterfeit documents. Hence, there is a need to digitalize the identity and documents to avoid such frauds.
\par
\textbf{Certificate Frauds.}
Fradulent diploma certificates have become a global phenomemon due to the rising demand of qualification requirement
in the job market~\cite{}. Based on national data in US, 6\% of bachelor degrees and 35\% of associate degrees are fake~cite{}.
Due to lack of transparency and digitalization of degree certificates by universities, the companies need to trust
the employee, and notraries. Hence, it is easily possible to produce and present fake diplomas. 
\par
\textbf{Blockchain Based Solution.}
To tackle the abovem entioned problems, a blockchain-based solution can be used to issue certificates and to verify issued
certificates. Figure~\ref{fig:identity-certificate} shows actors involved in such a blockchain solution. The issuing authorities
store the documents such as birth certificates, passport, driving license, diploma certificates directly into the replicated
ledger. These documents are owned by the respective individual and can control the access permission using the blockchain wallet.
The consumers of documents and certificates can get the permission from the individual and verify the document for various purpose
such as job appointment.
\par
\textbf{Data Privacy Violation.}\textit{
		Storing all sensitive document such as passport, diploma certificates in a replicated ledger could leak
		a lot of personal information about the individual. Hence, many of the individuals would not prefer to participate in
		such a blockchain network.}
